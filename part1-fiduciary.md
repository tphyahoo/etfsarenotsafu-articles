*Bitcoin ETFs Are Not Safu[^safu] — Part 1*

*~1,250 words / ~3 pages*

# When Is a Fiduciary Not a Fiduciary? What FTX and Prime Trust Actually Broke

Everyone already knows the FTX story, so it doesn't need much retelling. Alameda Research had a backdoor into customer deposits. Funds were never segregated the way a fiduciary is supposed to segregate them.[^terms] Roughly $10 billion in client assets turned out not to be there when people came looking. It was fraud, plainly and criminally. The lesson everyone took from it was blunt. Don't trust exchanges. Self-custody. Not your keys, not your coins.

That lesson is correct, but incomplete. It trains you to watch for *dishonesty*, a bad actor doing something obviously wrong on purpose. The failure that should worry a Bitcoin ETF holder more is the quiet one. It happened at a company with the word *Trust* right in its name: Prime Trust. No grand scheme, no backdoor, at least not at the start. A state charter made it a fiduciary on paper. A boring operational gap swallowed roughly $80 million of customer crypto anyway. (Whether the eighteen months it then spent quietly plugging that hole with *other* customers' deposits stays "boring" is something a bankruptcy court, and maybe eventually a prosecutor, is still unwinding.)

That's the pattern this series is about. Not fraud you can see coming, but the reassuring words the industry hands you: *Trust*, *proof of reserves*, *multi-party*, *audited*. Words that turn out to mean less than they sound. Prime Trust produced the cleanest example, straight from a bankruptcy court. A company can hold the legal *capacity* to be a fiduciary and still, when it counts, not be one. The reason has nothing to do with cryptography.

## The Wallet Event

Prime Trust was a Nevada-chartered trust company: regulated, licensed, the custody backend behind names like Binance.US, Celsius, Swan Bitcoin, and Strike. It had relied on a set of older "legacy" wallets, secured by engraved Cryptosteel backup plates, before becoming a Fireblocks customer in the summer of 2019. Here is where it actually went wrong. When new management ran into trouble creating wallets on Fireblocks, it *decided*, in January 2021, to route customer deposits back into those old legacy wallets. Not a founder building a hedge fund on customer money. But not a blameless "communication gap" either. A deliberate shortcut, and the decision the whole collapse traces back to.

By December 2021, Prime discovered it could no longer access those legacy wallets at all. It had lost the Cryptosteel plates needed to open them. It found out the way these things always surface. A customer requested a large withdrawal the company couldn't fulfill. So from December 2021 through March 2022, per the Nevada regulator's filing, "certain company employees" used more than $76 million in client fiat to buy replacement ETH and cover the withdrawals. The deficit eventually ran to roughly $82 million. That's commingling, the sin everyone was furious about at FTX. Except here it wasn't a hedge fund built on deposits. It was a company papering over an operational hole with other customers' money, hoping to grow out of it before anyone noticed.[^1]
## The contract said the quiet part out loud

Here's the detail that actually matters, and it's sharper than "Prime Trust breached its fiduciary duty." Which is true, but incomplete. When Nevada regulators shut Prime Trust down in June 2023, they found the company had "materially and willfully breached its fiduciary duties to its customers by failing to safeguard assets under its custody."[^2] That's the state, acting under its trust-charter authority, saying: you had the capacity to be a fiduciary, and you failed at it.

But when the bankruptcy court actually went looking for a fiduciary relationship in the paperwork, it found something else. Prime Trust's Master Services Agreement governed its relationship with the exchanges and platforms that used it as a custody backend. It stated plainly that the arrangement did "not create a partnership, franchise, joint venture, agency, fiduciary or employment relationship between the Parties." The court's July 2025 ruling made two separate, independent findings. First, the assets were "hopelessly commingled" to the point of being untraceable. Second, on the actual evidence in front of it, no trust relationship had ever been established between Prime Trust and the platforms relying on it. Not "Prime Trust broke its fiduciary promise." The court's reading was closer to this. There was never an enforceable fiduciary promise there to break. Not whatever the state regulator's charter-based finding said, and not whatever the word "Trust" in the company's name implied to everyone using it.[^3]

That's the actual answer to this piece's title. A state can grant a company the *capacity* to act as a fiduciary through a charter. And a private contract that company signs can, at the same time, define the *relationship* as something else entirely: a plain vendor arrangement, not a trust. Which one governs when the company collapses is a question for a bankruptcy judge, not a marketing page. In Prime Trust's case, the contract won.

## Who actually got hurt

The exchanges that built on top of Prime Trust (Swan Bitcoin, Strike, and others) marketed their own customers' funds as sitting in secure, regulated, fiduciary trust accounts. That's genuinely what they believed they'd bought. Some of them are now defendants in the bankruptcy's aftermath rather than just casualties of it. The PCT Litigation Trust now controls Prime Trust's remaining legal claims. It's suing Swan Bitcoin's parent company for roughly $970 million in Bitcoin and other assets. The argument runs like this. Because the underlying relationship was a debtor-creditor one, not a fiduciary one, the withdrawals Swan made in the run-up to the bankruptcy were preferential transfers, clawable back like any other creditor's early exit. Strike, Compass Mining, Fold, and Galaxy Digital have faced similar claims.[^4] Whatever these platforms told their own customers, the contract they'd actually signed said something colder.

## Where Coinbase deliberately built it differently

This is the part worth sitting with before moving on to the rest of this series. Coinbase Custody's legal architecture is explicitly built to close exactly this loophole. Its custodial agreement affirms fiduciary and bailee status rather than disclaiming it. It's structured under New York Banking Law, which doesn't let a limited-purpose trust company contract its way out of fiduciary duty the way Nevada allowed Prime Trust to. And it elects to treat custodied assets as "financial assets" under UCC Article 8. That's the same legal mechanism that makes a brokerage account's contents your property rather than the brokerage's. Coinbase cared enough about this specific legal question that it filed an amicus brief in the Prime Trust bankruptcy case, arguing in defense of the Article 8 financial-asset framework generally.[^5] That's a company that has clearly done its homework on this particular failure mode.

Which is exactly why it's the wrong place to go looking for Coinbase's next problem. FTX taught the industry to fear intentional fraud. Prime Trust taught it something more useful. A company can be well-regulated, mean well, and still lose customers' Bitcoin because of a boring, unglamorous detail nobody was scrutinizing. A wallet migration nobody closed the loop on. A contract clause nobody outside the legal department read closely enough. The thread holding the sweater together is never the part everyone's already watching. Coinbase has clearly reinforced the thread Prime Trust pulled. The rest of this series goes looking for the ones nobody's checking yet: the words the industry borrows to sound safe, the cryptographic machinery Bitcoin gets run through for someone else's convenience, and the policy layer that decides who can actually move your money, which no custodian discloses regardless of how airtight its fiduciary paperwork is.

[^terms]: Three "power words" that do the work in this piece. A **fiduciary** is legally bound to put another party's interest ahead of its own — the highest duty the law imposes, combining *loyalty* (no self-dealing, no using the beneficiary's assets for its own gain) and *care*. A **trust** is the arrangement in which one party (the trustee) holds assets for the benefit of another (the beneficiary), who keeps the beneficial ownership. A **bailee** merely holds someone else's property for a purpose and must return it — a coat check, a warehouse — without ever owning it. All three run on a **patchwork**, mostly state law, and it varies: Nevada's trust-company regime let Prime Trust contract *out* of a fiduciary relationship (as its MSA did); New York Banking Law does not let a limited-purpose trust company disclaim fiduciary duty the same way. So whether a company with "Trust" in its name actually owes you anything turns on its charter state *and* the contract you signed — which is the subject of this piece.

[^safu]: "Safu" is crypto-community slang for "safe," originating from a 2018 incident: Binance CEO Changpeng Zhao ("CZ") tweeted reassurances like "Funds are safe" (and, in a later post, "funds are safu") during a system upgrade. A YouTuber named Bizonacci turned this into a satirical viral video, "Funds Are Safu," and the misspelling stuck as shorthand across the industry — often used earnestly, sometimes ironically, given its origin as a reassurance made during a moment of user uncertainty. Binance later formalized "SAFU" as an actual name, the "Secure Asset Fund for Users," a real insurance-style reserve launched in July 2018.
