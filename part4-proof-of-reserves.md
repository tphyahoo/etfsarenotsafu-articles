# When Is a Proof of Reserves Not a Proof?

A Bitcoin ETF rests on one thing you cannot check for yourself. That the Bitcoin backing your shares actually exists. Seven years before the first spot ETF was approved, that problem was already solved, in writing, in an SEC filing.[^1] The 2017 Winklevoss Bitcoin Trust required its custodian to prove it actually held the coins: monthly "Proof of Control Examinations" by an independent auditor, plus an annual compliance certificate, delivered more often on request. A real, recurring, third-party check that the Bitcoin was there and the custodian controlled it. Not a promise. A proof.

Then the industry walked away from it. The Winklevoss application was rejected twice, in 2017 and 2018, but never over custody. Both rejections were about spot-market manipulation, the same fight that dragged on until Grayscale's 2023 court win forced the SEC's hand. Nobody ever ruled that monthly independent proof-of-control audits were unnecessary or too burdensome. By the time products actually launched in January 2024, that standard had simply vanished. Not removed, not overruled, just quietly dropped from whatever templates the eventual issuers built from. Neither the exchanges filing the paperwork nor the SEC staff reviewing it asked where it went.

So here is the state of the art in 2026, at the top of a market holding hundreds of thousands of Bitcoin. There is no real proof of reserves. What's offered instead comes in two flavors: a fake one from Bitwise (BITB), and none at all from BlackRock (IBIT).

## The Fake One: BITB

"Proof of Reserves" is not a marketing phrase. It has a precise origin. A 2013 exchange between Bitcoin Core's Greg Maxwell and Peter Todd, written up by Zak Wilcox in 2014.[^2] The construction pairs a Merkle tree of customer balances with a signed, on-chain proof of reserve control, letting any customer verify their own balance is counted, zero trust required in anyone. And it only works if the result is published to a shared public channel. Otherwise, as Wilcox warned, "an evil exchange might... provide a unique, entirely fabricated path-to-root to each customer."[^3] Public, permissionless, zero-trust. That's the whole point.

In July 2025, Bitwise announced "Proof of Reserves" for its BITB fund, a daily reconciliation performed by The Network Firm, an independent CPA.[^4] Progress, on its face. But look at what it replaced. Bitwise used to publish a list of roughly 90 wallet addresses that anyone could check on a block explorer, no trust required. The new "Proof of Reserves" swapped that for a CPA privately checking the addresses and issuing an attestation. No Merkle tree, nothing a customer can independently redo. The Network Firm's own disclosure gives the game away. Its system "independently queries" balances "from the relevant blockchain," and then, in the same breath, says the result "does not constitute an assurance that on-chain balances equal or exceed liabilities at any given point."[^5] An assurance that reserves cover liabilities is the one thing "Proof of Reserves" is supposed to mean. Bitwise traded a system nobody needed to trust for one that now requires trusting an auditor, and named it after the thing it isn't. Call it a BITB: a proof of reserves that audits everything except the reserves.

## None at All: IBIT

That's the fake version. The flagship doesn't even bother. An IBIT skips the proof rather than faking it.

With roughly $46 billion in AUM at the time of writing, BlackRock's IBIT is the largest spot Bitcoin ETF in existence, and there is no proof-of-reserves mechanism anywhere in its prospectus, fact sheet, or SEC filings. The gap is written into the contract itself. Coinbase's custodial agreements, publicly filed on EDGAR, spell out exactly what a client is owed: an electronic account statement, and compliance with UCC Article 8 duties as a "securities intermediary."[^6] What they do not contain, anywhere, is a right to independently verify holdings against the blockchain. No mandated addresses, no extended public key, no on-chain audit right. Coinbase reports a balance. The client reconciles its own books against that report. That is a bookkeeping relationship, not a cryptographic one. And the tool that would make it cryptographic is neither exotic nor risky. A watch-only *extended public key*, an `xpub`, lets a client derive every address a wallet will ever use and check the balances itself, in real time, on any block explorer, without ever holding a private key or the power to move a coin.[^xpub] Providing one would cost a custodian nothing but its opacity. Coinbase sends a statement. What circulates publicly instead is outsiders' guesswork. Firms like Arkham Intelligence claim to have identified IBIT's addresses by heuristic inference, not because anyone with the authority published them, and BlackRock has never confirmed the identification is even correct.[^6]

The obvious objection. Bloomberg's Eric Balchunas has said BlackRock runs its own node and checks balances against Coinbase's addresses nightly, shown to institutional clients on request but not published, for "spam" reasons.[^7] But "spam" explains a preference, not an impossibility. Unsolicited dust doesn't move a coin or cloud a balance, and a dusted address stays perfectly auditable. Bitwise, recall, published its own reserve addresses openly until it swapped them for a private attestation. Take the practice at face value anyway, and it still doesn't rebut the point. Which is that nothing in the filed contract obligates that check. It's a practice, which means Coinbase is sharing addresses through some undisclosed channel that no contract, regulation, or law requires. A courtesy, not a right. And a courtesy is one quiet, private policy email away from not existing. If Coinbase stopped tomorrow ("policy changed, for your security, trust us"), no canary sings to IBIT's shareholders, family offices, and retirees. There is no clause requiring anyone to tell them a practice stopped, because there was never a clause requiring it to exist.

This isn't hypothetical paranoia. It's the shape of Part 1's story. A sophisticated counterparty with informal visibility into a custodian's real condition doesn't ring the alarm for everyone else. It protects itself first. Swan Bitcoin withdrew roughly $970 million from Prime Trust in the months before it collapsed, on alleged insider knowledge, and is now facing a clawback suit for it.[^8] If BlackRock's private check ever showed something wrong, there is no reason to assume the public would hear about it before BlackRock's own money was safely out the door.

## The regulator asked, and accepted a shrug

So. Two ways to fail one test. A BITB proves the wrong thing, an IBIT proves nothing. 

None of this means the Bitcoin isn't there. But "a company this size has every incentive to get it right" is not a verification mechanism, it's a hope. Customers of Prime Trust had that hope too. 

Today's investors, pinning their hopes on the biggest Bitcoin product on Earth, get an IBIT, with less independently verifiable proof of reserves than plenty of exchanges the ETF world sneers at. Kraken, to name one, has for years published a Merkle-tree proof of reserves that lets each customer verify their own balance is in the count.[^kraken]

It isn't because nobody thought to ask. During the 2023 registration reviews, the SEC's own staff asked issuers, in writing, to "disclose whether any entity will be responsible for verifying the existence of the bitcoins," and made VanEck try again when its first answer fell short.[^9] That is the regulator asking the right question. Every issuer gave the same answer, and it wasn't an independent auditor or a proof of reserves. It was the custodian. Fidelity's prospectus stated the mechanism most plainly. The sponsor can "verify the existence of the Trust's bitcoin through information provided from the Custodian."[^9b] Verification derived from the very party being verified. The SEC accepted the closed loop. Whatever independent checks exist today arrived later and voluntarily, Bitwise's "proof of reserves" among them, the one we just took apart, never at the Commission's insistence. Seven years earlier it had a better answer sitting in a Winklevoss exhibit, and it never once fought to bring it back.

So why did it vanish? Not conspiracy. Reflex. For six years the SEC's entire fight over these products was about one thing: market manipulation, whether the listing exchange had a surveillance-sharing agreement with a regulated market of significant size. Custody was never the battlefield. In the very order rejecting the Winklevoss trust, the Commission waved through Gemini's NYDFS-supervised custody as adequate and moved on. So when Grayscale's 2023 court win finally broke the manipulation logjam, the products launched on the SEC's ordinary commodity-ETF terms, where the custody question is treated as answered the instant the coins sit with a qualified custodian and an auditor signs the annual books. Proof of reserves wasn't dropped so much as never held. It was one applicant's voluntary design, and it died with that applicant's self-custody model.

You could object that this is simply how bearer asset ETFs work, with the flagship gold ETF as the proof. The bullion behind GLD is verified by an independent auditor's twice-yearly physical count in an HSBC vault,[^gold] a number the ETF administrator takes on faith exactly like an account statement. And no gold ETF has lost its bullion to a custody failure (yet). But gold has an excuse Bitcoin doesn't. You cannot cryptographically prove a bar sitting in a London vault. You *can* prove a Bitcoin. It is the one asset in the building that never needed you to trust the vault, and the ETFs wrapped it in gold's trust-me packaging anyway.

## Not your keys, not your coins — the sequel

There's a version of this whole series easy to wave off as "well, that's why you self-custody." Fine. But the entities holding the largest piles of Bitcoin on Earth, the spot ETFs, institutional allocators, corporate treasuries, aren't going to self-custody. That's not the product they sell their investors. For them, "not your keys, not your coins" means something a level up: *know what you are actually owed, and refuse to accept "trust us" in place of proof.*

Prime Trust is the reminder of what happens when nobody asks until it's too late. A licensed fiduciary had a boring operational gap, a wallet migration nobody closed the loop on. And by the time a bankruptcy court sorted the wreckage, Bitcoin itself, the one asset built to prove exactly who owns what, got ruled "hopelessly commingled" with the estate.[^10] The technology didn't fail. Nobody was checking.

So here is the ask, plainly. ETF issuers should provide what this market already knew how to provide: independent, recurring proof that the reserves exist, of the kind the Winklevoss trust contractually promised a decade ago. Not "we're audited" as the end of diligence. The standard isn't exotic and it isn't new. It was here, and it was quietly allowed to lapse, and the regulator that fought these products for six years over market manipulation never once fought to bring it back. Institutions that demand independent audit trails for every other asset class they touch have accepted "trust the brand" for the one asset where the stakes, and the AUM, are highest. That's not diligence. It's habit. It's time to stop being polite about it.

[^safu]: "Safu" is crypto-community slang for "safe," originating from a 2018 incident: Binance CEO Changpeng Zhao ("CZ") tweeted reassurances like "Funds are safe" (and, in a later post, "funds are safu") during a system upgrade. Binance later formalized "SAFU" as an actual name, the "Secure Asset Fund for Users," a real insurance-style reserve launched in July 2018.
