# When Is a Party Not a Party?
Every launch key for the American nuclear arsenal sits inside one chain of command. Strategic Air Command holds all of them. That concentration — one organization, every key — was never the thing anyone feared. What makes it safe is the *two-man rule*: no single person can turn the key alone. It takes two officers, acting together, their stations set too far apart for one to reach both.

Hold that image, because it is the right way to think about who guards your Bitcoin — and not the way the custody industry wants you thinking.

Every institutional custodian sells you the same word. *Multi-party.* *Distributed.* *No single point of failure.* It paints the nuclear picture: a room full of people, each holding a piece, none able to act alone. Reassuring. Also not what *multi-party* means.

In multi-party computation — MPC, the cryptography behind Coinbase's custody and most of its competitors' — the "parties" are machines. Key-shards on servers. A signature gets produced when enough *computers* run a protocol together, fast and chatty, no human sitting inside — and none required at any point. The math guarantees no single *machine* can produce a signature.[^1] Whether two *humans* must agree to move the coins — the two-man rule — is a different question, and the cryptography does not answer it.

Now read how Coinbase actually describes it. To a retail user, MPC "splits a wallet's private key among multiple parties."[^1] To the institutions whose Bitcoin sits in Coinbase Custody Trust Company — the separately chartered subsidiary that holds IBIT — key shares are "distributed across multiple secure environments," a design that "eliminates single points of compromise."[^1] Multiple parties, multiple environments, no single point: every phrase is built to make you picture a crowd, and not one of them ever says who the parties are. Here is the plain thing none of them says: in that institutional vault, every one of those "parties" is Coinbase's own machine, in Coinbase's own building, under Coinbase's sole control. Multi-party is a claim about arithmetic, not about people — and arithmetic has no two-man rule.

So grant Coinbase the thing you assumed anyway: it holds all the keys itself.[^9] Fine. Concentration was never the point — the missile crews hold all their keys too. The question was never how many organizations hold the keys. It is how many *humans* it takes to move them — if any.

## Where the humans actually go

They don't vanish. They move to a different floor of the building — a separate layer bolted on top of the cryptography, called the policy or approval layer. This is where "does a human sign off on this withdrawal" gets decided, and it has nothing to do with the MPC threshold.

Coinbase documents this split in its own product. The cryptographic threshold is a fixed property of its machines — not yours to see or set. The human approvals are a business setting in a control panel: Coinbase Prime lets an institutional client "set the number of users needed to approve transactions," choosing "either a majority of approvers... or all approvers required," configured per transfer type and dollar size.[^2] The signing quorum is cryptography. The human quorum is a dropdown — set by the client, and shown to no one else.

Two different systems. The cryptography is mathematically guaranteed and loudly marketed. The human oversight is configurable, optional, and quiet. When a custodian says "multi-party," it is pointing at the first and letting you hear the second.

## Coinbase used to tell you about the humans

Here is the part that should bother an IBIT shareholder. Coinbase's disclosures about who guards the Bitcoin have gotten *less* specific about humans over time, not more.

**2016.** In the Shamir-secret-sharing era, Coinbase described an explicit human quorum: keys split and distributed to "geographically distributed" holders who "follow a protocol during key signing ceremonies to verify their identity," requiring "a quorum of key-holders to come together."[^3] Actual people, named as such.

**2021.** In a comment letter to the SEC, the language softened to "multiple independent actors."[^4] Ambiguous on purpose — actors can be machines. The one human the letter names is a "scribe," who "witnesses and documents every private key generation ceremony." A scribe does not approve and does not co-sign. A stenographer, not a guard.

**2024–2025.** In the current MPC-era marketing, the human-quorum language is gone. "No single point of compromise" now means no single *machine*.[^5] The one place Coinbase writes that "no single individual" can act maliciously, it is describing the *client's* organization — the ETF's approvers, configured by the ETF — not its own staff.[^5]

And yet the humans are still there — Coinbase just stopped putting them where you'd look. Press past the marketing to a 2024 interview and its own head of cryptography lays out the custodial key-split: "one share for the offline environment, **one share for human approvers**, and one share for each MPC server," all of it "inside Coinbase."[^lindell] So Coinbase knows exactly who its human approvers are, and how many. It simply won't tell you — not the number, not whether one is enough, not whether that share is even touched on a routine ETF withdrawal, as opposed to the key-generation ceremonies where its humans demonstrably do appear.[^3] Having conceded the humans exist, it discloses nothing you could hold it to. That is not a gap in the record. It's a choice.

And for IBIT specifically — the largest pile of institutional Bitcoin on Earth — the number of humans required to move it is disclosed nowhere. Not in the SEC filings. Not in the custody agreement, which points only to "Coinbase Custody's standard cold storage withdrawal procedures."[^6]

None of which proves no humans guard the coins. Some human-in-the-loop control probably exists. Coinbase Custody is a New York–chartered trust company, examined by NYDFS and audited under a private SOC 2. But look at what that regime actually gives you: NYDFS requires only that assets stay segregated and move at the customer's direction — no disclosed two-man rule, no set number of humans on a cold-storage withdrawal — and whatever the examiner does see is confidential by statute, off-limits to the public and shareholders alike.[^6] The reassurance reduces to trusting that an examiner sees what you are not allowed to. They used to tell you about the people. Now they tell you about the math.

And mind the gap between what the examiner certifies and what happens on the floor. NYDFS supervision blesses the architecture Coinbase *describes* — segregation, the shape of the controls, checked by periodic examination; it does not red-team whether a support contractor two continents away can be bribed. In May 2025 Coinbase disclosed that one had been: overseas customer-support contractors, paid as little as $200 a screenshot, sold roughly 70,000 *retail* customers' names, home addresses, government-ID images, and account-balance snapshots — a scheme Coinbase had known about since January and disclosed only after a $20 million extortion demand, at an expected cost in the hundreds of millions.[^breach] That breach hit retail support data — a different arm of the company from the trust that holds IBIT, with no coins or keys moved. But it is a live measure of the distance between *vetted* and *verified in practice*, and of how cheaply a Coinbase insider can be found and paid. The human layer a custodian won't quantify is the same human layer an adversary can shop for.

And be clear about what the missing thing is. A two-man rule is a human quorum — the safeguard that makes one organization holding every key tolerable, the one Strategic Air Command has. It is the one Coinbase has gone quiet about.

## What it looks like when the machines are perfect and the humans are not

Why does the human layer matter more than the cryptography? Because when multi-party custody fails, it is almost never the cryptography that breaks.

February 2025. Bybit loses roughly $1.46 billion — the largest crypto theft in history. The multisig math was never defeated. Attackers compromised the *interface*: the human signers opened their approval screen, saw a routine cold-wallet transfer, and approved it — while their hardware wallets were fed a malicious instruction they couldn't see. The signatures were valid. The humans were blind.[^7]

July 2024. WazirX loses about $230 million the same way — signers shown a benign transaction, approving a malicious one underneath it.[^8]

In both, the cryptography did exactly its job. It faithfully signed a fraudulent instruction, because the math never asks whether a withdrawal *should* happen. That question lives in the human layer — the one the marketing declines to quantify. Perfect signatures on a fraudulent order are still perfect signatures. And Bybit's signers weren't guarding a hot wallet; they were approving a move *out of cold storage* — the same kind of transfer that walks IBIT's Bitcoin out the door.

## The counterargument

The usual reply is that cryptographic enforcement beats human process: a machine doesn't get tired, doesn't get careless, can't be bribed the way a guard can. True — and beside the point. A machine that can't be bribed will still faithfully sign whatever instruction clears the policy layer. That is precisely what happened to Bybit: the cryptography was flawless, and it flawlessly signed a theft. The failure modes that actually empty a custodied vault — a fraudulent withdrawal that passes policy, an insider at or above the threshold, a coerced signer, a court order — are the ones a machine threshold does nothing about, because the math never asks whether a withdrawal *should* happen. The two-man rule isn't there to make the signing stronger. It's there so that more than one human has to look, and agree, before the flawless machine does exactly as it's told. The starkest version isn't fiction: in October 1962, a Soviet submarine's nuclear launch required three officers to concur — and one of them, Vasili Arkhipov, refused. The torpedo stayed in its tube.[^arkhipov] The machine would have fired. The second human is the one who asks whether it *should*.

None of which "multi-party" addresses. The word tells you about the machines. It tells you nothing about how many independent humans stand between a fraudulent instruction and your Bitcoin — and at Coinbase, those humans work for one company, in a number it won't disclose.

## The question to actually ask

Not "is it multi-party?" Everyone is multi-party; the word is free, and it only ever promised you machines. Ask instead: *how many humans must agree to move it?* And notice how generous that question already is. This whole piece has asked for *two*. Coinbase's cryptographer confirms the *design* has one — a "human approvers" share — but not whether it's two, whether one suffices, whether it guards a routine IBIT withdrawal, or who inside Coinbase even decides. The architect describes a capability; the policy lives on some other floor; and no one on any floor will give you the number. Strategic Air Command can tell you exactly how many humans it takes to turn the most dangerous keys on Earth. Coinbase, about the largest pile of institutional Bitcoin on Earth, has been asked — and will only say: *some*.

A party, in the end, is not a person. Coinbase's cryptography is a room full of machines that never disagree. Whether a single human can walk them to a fraudulent instruction lives in a layer they don't publish. You're shown that the machines are plural. You're left to assume the people are.

## Is it even as safe as gold?

You could call all of this just how ETFs work, and point to gold. But gold is the comparison that indicts the Bitcoin ETF, not the one that excuses it. To move the bullion behind a gold fund, someone has to move *metal* — an authorized participant, a vault at HSBC, serial-numbered four-hundred-ounce bars, logged, insured, and slow.[^bullion] That human-in-the-loop isn't a policy anyone chose; it's physics. You cannot drain a London vault with a message from a laptop.

Bitcoin owes physics nothing — which is its genius in your own hands and its exposure in a custodian's. The safeguard gold gets *for free* — humans, unavoidably, in the loop — the Bitcoin ETF has to *engineer*, as policy. And a policy can be configured thin, kept secret, and, as Bybit proved, walked straight past. Gold gives you a floor made of atoms: no one can take it away, and no one can hide it from you. The Bitcoin ETF poured its floor from math and human process — and then turned the lights off over it.

[^safu]: "Safu" is crypto-community slang for "safe," originating from a 2018 incident: Binance CEO Changpeng Zhao ("CZ") tweeted reassurances like "Funds are safe" (and, in a later post, "funds are safu") during a system upgrade. A YouTuber named Bizonacci turned this into a satirical viral video, "Funds Are Safu," and the misspelling stuck as shorthand across the industry — often used earnestly, sometimes ironically, given its origin as a reassurance made during a moment of user uncertainty. Binance later formalized "SAFU" as an actual name, the "Secure Asset Fund for Users," a real insurance-style reserve launched in July 2018.
